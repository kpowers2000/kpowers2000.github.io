Coming to You Live from Planet Earth

When the aliens came, no one expected them to laugh at us. Yet, as I massage the maroon mark forming on my forehead—don’t text and walk, kids—the alien in my apartment complex lets out a cackle. It is always the same, slow cackle. That intolerable, unvaried, mind-numbing, racket of a sound. Its constant resonance reverberates through your skull. And you can’t forget that smile. Or the way that wistful smirk, the first of two departures from his ordinarily vacant expression, wanes as his orifice drops to a wallow for the second. It is as if he longs to loop back in time, with his memory of recent events wiped, so he can witness the asinine downfall of your exploits.

“OHAHA, watch where you are going there, Mr. Mustard, OHAHA,” Zima said in his gravel voice. You spill mustard on your shirt and corduroys one time, and suddenly that becomes your identity. It's always this way with the aliens. They name you after the embarrassing mistakes you make in front of them

I struggle to slide open the doors. When they finally budge, one slips off the damaged track. The doors are supposed to be automatic, but I failed to notice the yellow tag across the pane with the words OUT OF ORDER sprawled across. Hence the sweltering maroon mark that has now swelled to the size of a plum on my forehead.

I massage this maroon bump on my forehead as I step out into the chilly February air. Zima laughs again as I struggle to close the (no longer) automatic doors. I finally get the door back on the broken track.

“Fuck off, Mr. Extraterrestrial Jelly Bean.” Zima starts laughing again. A smile creeps up as I exit the door, but my jaw tightens, grin turning to grimace as I am greeted by the chilly February air. …

Lastly, I am focused on improving my professional career. I especially encourage you to look at the letters of recommendation found on the “Letters of Outstanding Improvement” page of this website.

Zima—the name I gave to the alien living in the main lobby of my apartment complex —moved in the same day I moved did: T+0:0:0. Well, technically, I was just moving back after a regretful two month hiatus.

It was also the day I came out to my Christian parents. You see, there was this whole Y2K vibe going around, and I didn’t want to live—or possibly die—with regret anymore. (My partner of four years, George, also dumped me that day. He kicked me out of his apartment too. I guess he didn’t want to live with regret anymore either.)

Coming out to them went as expected, given that they are the kind of Christian that wrote in for Paul Ryan in the 2020 election: less homophobic, more homo-ignorant. Surprisingly, my parents’ awkward hugs and very descriptive questions were probably the least awkward part of my day. It was, after all, the same day the aliens arrived, though that was the least eventful part of the day. …

The car alarm blares after I open the door. I swear and hurry to put the key into the ignition and twist it. The car engine turns over after (quite a few) nervous ticks and the alarm cuts out. Frostnipped, I press my hands against the vents and blast the heat. As I wait for the engine to heat up, I see Sienna in the main lobby elevator. She lives directly across from me on floor two of Sunhills 74th and has a dozen cats. (She also keeps three chickens hidden in her back room, but I keep quiet in exchange for a basket of fresh eggs each month)

Surprisingly, even she isn’t single. She’s married to Manuel, a young French clockworker. They had been pen-pals for several years, and she would talk about him incessantly. I always thought he was a fictional character taken from one of the trashy romance novels I overhear her reading to her favorite cat Gorjina Clemens, a cylindrical casket of pumpkin orange fur. But, on the day of The Arrival, I was proven wrong when a handsome senior with salt and pepper hair and a silver handlebar mustache knocked on my door.

“I em looking for eh Sienna Clem’mehn,” he was tense and spoke with a French twinge. “My name is eh Manuel,” he added after a brief pause, eyeing me up and down, “her lover.” “She’s, uh, across the hall,” I stuttered. “Right there,” I pointed straight across to the door swallowed by motivational quotes and arts & crafts projects.

He turned around swiftly, his posture strong. I stood gaping, and noticed the bulge (in his pocket) that had the outline of a wedding box. I closed the door, momentarily forgetting my own heartbreak just a few hours earlier. Later, I would find out he proposed on the spot. They spent their Arrival Day waiting in an unbelievably long line to get married at the courthouse. …

“PUTE,” Sienna swears in French. I look up through the car windshield to watch as she shifts her wait to hobble on one leg and check the bottom of the other. A glint of pink tells me she stepped in gum. I am sure, somehow, that Zima is behind it. She is surprisingly posed for someone in their seventies—I need to do more yoga.

I have this recurring dream that she is the thirteenth cat in her apartment. In it, her nails stretch to talons, and whiskers sprout from her nose. Then Manuel enters as a stripper in a baker’s uniform holding a box of a bagels. “I only have 12 bagels in here, madame, it is no baker’s dozen.” His stoic body slumps in shame.

“Well a baker needs his thirteenth hole.” Sienna purrs before she knocks the bagels out of his hands. Her talons scratch his face as she grabs him by the silver ends of his handlebar mustache and pulls him in for a kiss.

A crash rings out, though it is muffled as it passes through glass to my car. Felix, a Software Engineer at a failing tech start-up that promises to “change the world more than the aliens did”, had bumped into Sienna, spilling his coffee and knocking her down. I brace, knowing what will come next.

Zima’s body is all static, save for what could only be described as a mouth, though I can’t be sure. I have never seen him eat with it—though I have never seen an alien eat anything for that matter. I’m not sure it has any purpose besides projecting his laughter. He doesn’t try to help, just stands there laughing.

Well, I’m not sure if stand is the right word—he doesn’t have any legs. I guess he sits upright, templing his amorphous shape so his facial features rest at the crux. Think Jabba the Hutt if he had semi-transparent flesh, like lime-flavored gelatin. One look at him and I could never eat Jell-O again. Everyone else agreed; they went out of business a year after The Arrival. No one gave a shit—except PETA. They celebrated.

The laughing fades as Felix helps Sienna up off the ground. And with the car warmed, I set off to work, turning on the radio as I pull out of the parking lot. “...so far there are no confirmed American casualties. The UN and Coexist have both issued statements urging countries to hold their artillery, hoping for peace…” The radio gives to static. I change stations.

“This just in, an anonymous source from Washington has leaked information to the press, describing top-secret plans for the U.S., China, and Russia to send nuclear warheads at the Mothership this evening.”

“But Russia wanted the glory, so they launched them four hours early. Here is Dr. Dan Simons, a nuclear physicist who is going to tell us what happened. Dr. Simons, care to fill us in?” “As the viewers are likely aware of now, Russia launched all 6,285 of their atomic bombs at the aliens’ home base this evening. The warheads hurtled at speeds in the upper range of Mach 2 toward the spaceship. At that speed, it would take three minutes and forty-two seconds for the first launch to reach the mothership…” Static blocked the radio as I drove below a bridge.

“And what does that mean?” “Basically, stopping these rockets after launch is impossible by any standards we know.” “Then, pardon me, doctor, but what the hell just happened?” “It seems that the Russian warheads… I don’t know how to say this any other way… It appears the Russian warheads… stopped.” “What do you mean?”

“Satellite observations show that the warheads appear to have stopped midair and reversed trajectory. Scientifically, I can think of no explanation—” “This just in, Climatologists are witnessing a never before seen phenomena. Trade winds Have shifted, creating a cyclone around Russia, trapping any potential radiation in the Red Nation—hold on—more breaking news: the presidents of the U.S. and China have issued a joint statementing recalling their—” The radio shuts off as I take the key out of the engine. I nod to Derek as he buzzes me in. I push the elevator button and wait for the doors to open.

“How’s your day going?” Derek asks. “Like any other day. Anything interesting happening today.” “Randy looked pretty riled up this morning. Angela baked some sugar-free cookies—don’t eat them, they taste like baking soda—oh, and we got an alien in the office again. This time, its a screecher.”

“Just one?” I inquire. It's strange enough seeing one of the E.T.-types indoors, but to see one alone is almost unheard of. “Just one,” Derek replies, “though she’s on the third floor today,” he adds, knowing I work on the second. Not that it means much, I think. E.T.s don’t stay in the same place for long. “And Gorja?”

“Who?” Derek refuses to give an alien a name. I sigh, “The grumbler.” “Haven’t seen him since T+4:3:12.” Two weeks. He really must be gone. Gorja moved in next to the second-floor water cooler on day T+0:0:4, the forth day after The Arrival. He’s a Jabba-type, like Zima. His departure wouldn’t be too strange if he were an E.T., but he’s a Jabba. Jabbas—unlike E.T.s—tend to stay at their home base, usually in an indoor public space, though I heard one moved into a private family home on 83rd. It completely tanked their property value.

I should explain: there are two types of aliens; I call them Jabba’s and E.T.s. Some think they are the different sexes, others think they are just coexisting species, joining forces for the shared bond of amusement at an inferior species’ continual incompetence. We aren’t exactly sure which would be male or female, or if the terms “male” and “female” even apply.

Everyone also seemed to develop their own unique terminology to differentiate the two. In Chicago, they call them Screechers and Grumblers; in Boston, they call them the Skeezahs and Baboes; the southwest calls them Puckets and Pansies. In Philly, when I visited family, they were both called jawn. My cousin swears you can tell the difference, depending on context and pronunciation. Personally, I like to give individual names to each of the aliens I meet. Call me a Symbiote.

“I hear that’s been happening more often recently,” Derek chimed in, breaking the awkward silence as I still wait for the elevator to come down. The sign above the elevator has had the number 3 glowing in red for a suspiciously long time now.

“What?” I say, having lost the train of conversation. “Screechers aren’t walking around like they used to,” Derek continues to ramble, “Grumblers’ leaving their nests. People seem to think their numbers are dwindling. Some reckon they might be going home, after all this time.” He waits for me to respond, but I say nothing. Derek always thinks the aliens are about to leave. He does have a point, though. In the past few months, the number of aliens in the streets and public spaces has dwindled.

“Strange,” I say. “Been hearing stories of them growlers attacking people. Concerning stuff. Best start carrying my gun around. Military is gonna have us taking up arms any day now.” I doubt it. “I’ll be on it,” I lie. I consider telling him I don’t think his Remington 870 could kill them. It would probably just go straight through them. Or worse, it would bounce off them the same way the Russian nukes bounced off the mothership. “Elevator seems to be busted,” Derek observes. The light atop still reads floor 3. “Best take the stairs.”

As I climb the steps, I reach into my pockets to take out my government-issued earplugs. The earplugs reduce high frequency and repetitive noise. So, you can have a conversation with a colleague just fine, but the high pitched terror that is an E.T.’s laughter becomes just a muted laughter.

After taking out my wallet, phone, and keys, I reach in to find there is nothing left. Then, it dawns on me that I left the earplugs on the counter. You don’t want to be exposed to an E.T.’s laugh for long: unlike the deep, throaty cackle of a Jabba, ET’s laughs are high-pitched, like Spongebob crossed with the Wicked Witch. A few days spent around an E.T., even with ear protection, and you’ll have migraines and tinnitus for weeks. I consider taking the day off, but I’m one less paycheck away from the Instant Ramen Diet.

A high-pitched shriek pierces my open canals. I can’t see where it's coming from, but I have a strange feeling it’s directed at me. I reach the top of the steps and open the door to the second floor. Randy is making a scene:

“This is ridiculous. This is a violation of my free speech.” “Look, Randy, HR just wants the workplace to be a politically-free zone. You have a right to your opinions, we just ask that you keep provocative clothing out of the office.” Yesterday, Randy wore a shirt that said “Make Our Country Alien-Free Again” with a picture of a red-necked hillbilly in an AR-15 blowing the heads off a couple of ETs and a Jabba. “This is a load of pig shit. I bet it was Susan. John, did Susan say something?

“I am not at liberty to discuss who filed the complaint.” Randy and Susan are neighbors in the office, situated in the front right corner by the largest window. They dated a few years back, before the aliens came, but things went sour. They were at each other's throats for a while, both refusing to give up the best cubicles in the office. But when a Jabba came into the office, their daily bickering fell to a minimum. Everyone, even Susan and Randy, was too afraid to make a scene, not wanting to hear that wretched laugh at the behest of someone’s embarrassment.

But after Gorja left, HR complaints skyrocketed. Without a Jabba to threaten the peace, kindness became currency, and the greedy hoarded it. And now that Susan was eight months pregnant with her new boyfriend's baby, Randy is aggravated and fresh out of spare change.

“That alien-loving piece of shit. I bet that baby of hers is gonna come out half-alien. Probably got knocked up by that fat, slimy freak in our office.” I can’t take my eyes off the opal blue vein protruding from Randy’s forehead. “I’ve already had sex with one fat, slimy freak in our office. I won’t make the same mistake twice,” Susan retorts.

Randy scoffs, unable to think of a clever comeback. “Susan has a Symbiote sticker on her water bottle. Can I file an HR complaint for that?” “Sir that is a bit different then—

“I fucking knew it. Bunch of Symbies in this office. No respect for our species, wanting to let these damned aliens laugh at us in the hopes they’ll give us some of their techs. The Symbie politicians say they may give us 1000G internet. Maybe, they will. Maybe, pretty soon they’ll start slipping in some nanotech. Maybe, next thing we know we got probes up our asses, mind-controlling us to trip on our shoelaces and spill our coffee. All so they can get a few extra laughs.”

The Symbie politicians say they may give us 1000G internet. Maybe, they will. Maybe, pretty soon they’ll start slipping in some nanotech. Maybe, next thing we know we got probes up our asses, mind-controlling us to trip on our shoelaces and spill our coffee. All so they can get a few extra laughs.”

“I don’t think they would do that.” The words escape me before I realize it. Hesitantly, I elaborate: “mind-control would take the laugh out of it. “So you’re an expert on aliens now, Arthur?” I shrug, feeling the weight of the entire floor’s attention. “They think its funny when we embarrass ourselves on our own volition.”

I shrug, feeling the weight of the entire floor’s attention. “They think its funny when we embarrass ourselves on our own volition.” “You’ve been at this company for twelve years and haven’t even gotten a promotion yet. The intern makes more than you.” John tries to put a stop to the conflict, “This is unacceptable. Randy and Arthur, I am going to have to write—” Everyone winces as an infuriating giggle reverberates in the office space. I try to cover my unprotected ears. An E.T. stands by the elevator. She must have taken the elevator down, and from the looks of it, she must have been there a while. She is staring directly at me.

Her effect is instantaneous. Randy goes silent. Not even he would say a word against an alien. We have been corralled like sheep, like fidgeting kids afraid to upset the teacher, and we are quiet as the E.T. scans the classroom for misbehavior. The daily gossips and bickerings are absent.

By T+15, the only interruptions to the quiet were a few muted murmurs and some scattered snickerings from Fobja, the name I’ve given our unwanted colleague. At T+16, Fobja finally leaves, and everyone in the office breathes a bit easier for the last stretch of the day.

At T+17, the workday finally ends, and I get into the elevator with Isabel, a senior manager at the firm. Just before the doors close, Fobja slips in. Horrified, I try to make a quick escape, but it is too late: the elevator doors slide shut as I slam against them. I try to move away but realize my left sleeve is caught between the doors. Fobja lets out a SpongeBob laugh. I realize I can’t reach the buttons.

“Which floor?” Isabel asks straight faced as I try to wedge my fingers between the elevator doors. Its the end of the day. Of course I want to go to ground floor. I almost let out a smartass response, but I don’t: the laughter is relentless. The violent ringing slams my ears as my head feels like it is splitting in two.

“One,” I respond, attempting to mimic her nonchalant tone. She reaches around the alien and presses the button. As the elevator descends, my left arm is pulled upwards, causing my shirt to untuck, exposing my (only slightly hairy) belly. I fight to keep my back to the door, and Isabel and I both act as though nothing is out of the ordinary. Fobja’s laughter only gets louder as I am raised to my tippy toes, but Isabel acts as if this twisted posture of mine, like a mannequin strewn upon a string, is a perfectly normal sight. Then, without a word, Isabel hands me her earplugs. The Fobja’s laughter suddenly stops,. I steal a glance her way. She looks defeated. Finally, the door opens, and I stumble out, almost losing my footing. Fobja, still wallowing in self-pity, fails to notice my clumsiness as she sweeps by.

Without a word, Isabel and I part ways. I exit the building. The midday sun made the outside air slightly more tolerable, but I still walk briskly to the car, start the engine (no trouble this time), and blast the heat.

On the ride home, I see a Jabba at a red light. His eyes follow a homeless woman as she weaves through the lanes. She holds a sign asking for money, and goes up first to a silver Buick a few cars ahead of me. The driver, a middle-aged man in a leather jacket and sunglasses, doesn’t see the alien. He shakes his head, stern and unforgiving, but his expression cracks when the cackle rings out from the Jabba in his blind spot. The driver rolls down his window, having changed his mind, and hands the vagrant a fistful of cash. The other cars in the lane, myself included, follow suit, not wanting to aggravate the alien. When the light turns green, the Jabba leaves his post.

A few minutes later, I turn on 74th street and pull into my apartment. I wiggle a finger in my ears as I get out of the car, the the now well-acquainted ringing drilling into my ears. I lower my head as I walk, thankful for Isabel’s earplugs to block the sound of construction going on across the street.

Then I hit my head at the entrance for the second time today, and the earplugs fall out. Sound rushes through my ears. Except I do not hear the rattle of the brittle glass doors. Rather, I hear a squishing sound, and I realized I slammed against something soft, slimy, something with the consistency of Jel-O. I open my eyes to semi-transparent green flesh. I take a step back and look up to see Zima looking down at me, his gel-like body wrapped around one of the glass doors. We make eye contact and stand in silence for a few seconds. Why is he standing like that? The realization comes slowly, and despite the ache in my ears, I can’t help but smile. “You’re stuck?” I question. “OHAHA no, of course not, petty human.” Zima tries to sound calm, but I detect a faint quiver.

“Bullshit. You got stuck in the door.” My smile widens. “Did you not see the OUT OF ORDER sign, Zima?” I question mockingly. “Shut up, Colonel Mustard.” “Or what, Mr. Bellhop?” We stare at each other in silence for a moment. Then, it starts. First, it is a soft bubble in my chest, but soon it swells. My lungs press hard against my ribs until suddenly, the bubble pops, and a wave of laughter erupts out. I laugh until I am bent over, until I am one knee to the ground, trying to remember how to breathe.

“You look ridiculous,” I force out between gasps for air. His body is mangled on the hinges. As I get control of my breathing, I am surprised to find I wasn’t the only one laughing. Zima had joined in. A second wave of howls rolls through me. Minutes have passed, but it feels like hours. My vision begins to tunnel, but, in the midst of my convulsions, I manage to pry open the door, squishing it past his gel-like flesh until, with a final nudge and a squeak of its hinges, I manage to separate Zima and door. His elastic body snaps back to its typical loosely templed shape. My breath steadies, and I take in a large gulp of air, restoring oxygen to my lungs.

Zima and I look at each other in silence. For a second, I think I see a slight sadness in his eyes, but it quickly fades. I step aside and let him pass. “See you later, Mr. Bellhop” “Goodbye, Arthur.”

The next day, I slide open the broken automatic doors and head to my car. As I wait for the engine to heat up, I look through the windshield to the main lobby of Sunhills 74th Apartments. It’s empty, the only evidence of its previous inhabitant is a faint puddle of green slime leading out to the door.Some moments later, I put the car into reverse and turn on the radio and begin flipping channels.

He claimed that the QUOTE Russian nukes scared them off ENDQUOTE.”

Use promo code BCG105 for 10% off…”

Dr. Lee, do you mind elaborating?” “Yes. You see, I believe they were running experiments on us that only a higher mind could comprehend. These experiments likely took place in our sleep, in my up-and-coming book, Poking and Prodding…” I shut the radio off. I think they just wanted a laugh.
