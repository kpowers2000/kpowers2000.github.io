Published Work

“Hitting time of Brownian motion subject to shear flow,” Involve, a Journal of Mathematics Vol 15 No 1.

I have one notable pushled research paper of which I made a significant contribution. The article, “Hitting time of Brownian motion subject to shear flow,” was published in Involve, a Journal of Mathematics. Over one summer, some students and I mathematically modelled coral reproduction under the mentorship of Professor Alexander Kiselev, a professor in Duke’s Department of Mathematics.

For my role, I led the software portion, developing code in MATLAB to run simulations on the collective motion of the coral sperm and egg reproduction process. I included several different parameters in my simulation: ocean current, shear flow of the liquid layers, random Brownian motion of the sperm, chemotaxins released by the egg to attract the sperm, and temperature of the ocean. One goal of the study was to see how changing temperature and ocean currents caused by climate change might affect coral reproduction. Though our results are not conclusive, we found that increasing current speeds and temperatures negatively effected the probability that the sperm found the egg in our target-seeking simulations.

To model the collective motion of coral reproduction, we developed target-seeking software in MATLAB that uses partial differential equations, computational fluid dynamics, and coral data to simulate a sperm tracking an egg in a typical aquatic environment. We defined a sucess as the sperm sucessfully finding the egg in a certain timeframe, and analyzed how the probability of a successful reproduction changed when we manipulated certain variables such as shear flow, temperature, and current speed.

“Black Hole - Mathematically Accurate Stereographic Projection,” STL. While this is not a published paper, I spend my fall semester of 2022 helping Professor Hubert Bray modernize his teaching plan for his Geometry course. As part of this independent study, I made laser prints of different geometric projections and 3D prints of various mathematical concepts. In one such print, I 3D printed a black hole based on recently published equations on their projections. Since black holes are 4-dimensional objects—the x, y, z spacial coordinates and time—I used the mathematical formula for the widely accepted 3-dimensional stereograhic projection of the black hole to render my 3D print. The projection condenses the spacial coordinates into 2 dimensions by projecting points from a spatial sphere onto a plane from the north pole of the sphere. After creating the STL file for this 3D print, I published it publicly to thingiverse. Since publishing, I have scoured the web and found no previous renditions. Thus, to my knowledge, I am the first to publish a mathematically accurate stereographic projection of a black hole.

Community Engagement

Giving back to my community is really important. Despite being an openly queer man, I come from a place of numerous privileges from my race, physical and mental health, socioeconomic status, and gender expression, among others. So, I do what I can to give back to the community

Some simple things I do to give back are choosing to put my investments in socially conscious business, donating blood every 56 days for the last 2 years, and signing up for the Gift of Life Marrow Registry. If you qualify, donating blood is a simple way to make a huge impact on those in your community who need it. Just a single donation can save up to 3 lives. There is a constant blood donation shortage, and donating can help vulnerable populations. In particular, your donation can go to those diagnosed with cancer, burn and accident victims, and patients of cardiovascular surgery. Another simple way of giving back is to sign up for the Gift of Life Marrow Registry. You are not committing to anything by signing up, and all it takes is a simple cotton swab on the inside of your cheek. If you match with someone, you are likely to be one of the only matches they will get, in which case you could very well be the only person who could save their life.

Some of the more involved things I do that take up more significant amounts of my time include serving as the president of oSTEM@Duke, being treasurer of the Engineering Graduate Student Council (EGSC), Substitute Teaching at Raleigh Charter High School, and serving as a teaching assistant in the math and engineering departments at Duke University.

oSTEM stands for Out in Science, Technology, Engineering, and Mathematics. It is a 501(c)(3) non-profit professional association. It is the largest chapter-based organization for LGBTQ+ people in STEM. I am president of the chapter at Duke University. While oSTEM is a national organization, the organizations real strength stems from its grassroots initiatives. We fundraise, plan and execute our own events, provide a community for queer students at Duke, and foster queer solidarity and resilence in the South. Then, we come together nationally and regionally, bringing together the different chapters a few times a year for conferences, business development summits, and other community building events.

As treasurer of EGSC, I handle five-figure finance and budget operations for the largest graduate engineering student body. I help with planning social events, research emporiums, hold contests, and help run recruiting for the incoming graduate student body at Duke Pratt School of Engineering

The community engagement that has perhaps been the most rewarding, however, has been my current stint as a substitute teach for a local high school. Education is important, and I have chosen to have an active role in teaching the next generation. Therefore, I have put in more than 100 hours of service in the last year alone as a substitute teacher in various courses in math, science, English, and history.

In addition to substitute teacher, I have taken part in other education initatives. I currently hold the record in the Math department at Duke University as the student with the most teaching assistantships to date at 20 math courses. This does not include the semester I worked in the Math Help Room, holding open office hours for any student in any of the introductory calculus or linear algebra courses.

I am also twice a teaching assistant for Professor Peggy Matson in the course Business Fundementals for Engineers in which I have stood out as an exceptional teaching assistant, known for my initiative and determination to go exceed expectations. On top of my usual teaching duties, I have led tech support, written software, and done design work to elevate the class to a new level. You can see my software and design work on the GitHub and Powers Design pages on this website respectively. Furthermore, you can navigate to my Letters of Recomendation page to read a stellar review from Professor Peggy Matson attesting to my outstanding performance as a teaching assistant.

It is important for us to give back to the community with what we can, whether that be through our skills, time, or financial support. People have advantages and disadvantages, but we can each utilize our advatages to help others and foster a better society.
